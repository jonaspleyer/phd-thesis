\subsection{Cellular Reproduction \& Death}
Many cellular systems are in a constant dynamic state of turnover.
Epithelia need to balance cell division and death in order to maintain a constant size.
This can be achieved by extrusion where a single cell is pushed out of the tissue layer in densely
crowded areas~\cite{Eisenhoffer2012}.
Once the cell leaves the confiment, apoptosis is triggered.
Another mechanism is via cell competition where neighboring cells inform each other aobut their
metabolic activity and growth and lower performing cells are then instructed to
die~\cite{Amoyel2014}.
Precisely timed cell death is also observed in pattern formation processes such as the formation of
digits and limbs~\cite{Zou1996,Weatherbee2006}.
These examples make it clear that the methods which we develop need to be able to naturally account
for variable number of cellular agents.

\subsection{Individual-Based Mathematical Treatment}
It is clear that this approach targets models on the mesoscopic scale such that not every
fundamental effect can be taken into account.

\section{The Cellular Space}
\label{subsec:cellular-space}
This section develops a formal notion of how to describe a collection of unique cellular agents.
To motivate our constructions, we leverage known biological behaviour as a template.
By formulating these concepts in abstract mathematical terms, we enable ourselves to cover a wide
variety of \acp{cabm} and in turn cellular systems.

The next subsections will discuss how the concepts of cellular uniqueness, division and death can be
interpreted using an individual-based approach.
We will also briefly discuss some practical aspects such as construction of unique identifiers and
tracability of cells in applications.

\subsection{A Cellular Identity Crisis}
Our mathematical formalism aims to describe a variable amount of cells.
In order to properly distinguish cells from each other we aim to provide an identification scheme
that discriminates between each individual entity.
The following examples should be viewed as thought-provoking background information and motivation
for the coming definitions.

\subsubsection{Tracability}
Cellular systems are characterized by the fact that---leaving practical limitations aside---cells
are tracable through time and space.
This means that the trajectory of any cell can be followed precisely over the course of its
lifetime.
Moreover, this involves keeping track of its lineage which starts either at the initial time point
of the experiment or when the cell is newly created, e.g. after the division of their mother cell.
These ideas tie into the concept of \textbf{Cellular Continuity} which states that any cell arises
purely from other pre-existing cells~\cite{Mazzarello1999}.
But this is not merely a genetic relation which is supported by the observations that new membranes
are only formed by extending and dividing existing ones~\cite{Harold2005} and mitochondria need to
be inherited during cell division~\cite{Westermann2010}.
The cell's lifetime ends when it reaches a final fate such as death or division.
Furthermore, once a cell has ceased to exist, it will not reemerge by any other mechanism.
These conceptually simple ideas already capture important core ideas of how to identify cells.
On the application side, the process of identifying cells in microscopic (3D) images is known as
spatial segmentation~\cite{Cutler2022,Stringer2020,Hardo2022}.
These methods can be extended to dynamically track cells over the course of time but the additional
complexity still poses substantial problems for existing
solutions~\cite{Maka2014,Maka2023,Ulman2017,Wen2021}.

\subsubsection{Identification via Cellular Properties}
One could be tempted to identify each cell by its physical and internal properties.
However, while this idea is supported by the concept of \textbf{Cellular Uniqueness} which states
that no two cells will ever be identical, even when sharing equal genetic material, it leaves out
practical challenges of modeling such systems.
To illustrate this, we perform a thought experiment.

We assume that a cell is defined by its position on a lattice grid $\mathbb{Z}$ together with some
form of discrete internal state.
The dynamics of the system are irrelevant for our considerations but we assume that multiple cells
can occupy the same position.
If two cells with matching internal states sit at the same location, they can not be
distinguished from each other if no additional properties of both cells are tracked.
One could be tempted to interpret this within the scope of the principle of indistinguishable
particles which is employed across statistical physics often attributed to Dirac~\cite{Dirac1926}.
Quantum systems in particular are known to utilize this assumption for elementary particles such as
bosonic ones where exchange of two particles leaves the wave function
unchanged~\cite{Griffiths2018}.
However, said assumption is in general not valid for such complex entities as biological cells.
The only reason that these cells seem indistinguishable is that our reduced model captures only
coarse cellular properties.
Slight model adjustments could lead to a proper discrimination between the two entities.

But while the specific cellular representation within our model is a key question for any scientist,
it is not the only source of identification challenges.
If we set out to directly compare model results to biological observations, a similar problem
emerges.
We now omit the assumption of discreteness in our model and consider a situation in which simulation
results are being compared to experimental observations.
The model is initialized with a known state given by experimental data and compared at a later time
point.
Such a comparison will in practice always require a notion of distance as a metric $d(\cdot,\cdot)$
in the space of our observable quantity.
We set out to link experimental cell states to states produced by our model but assume that the
chosen metric only involves physical cellular properties such as the position of the cell or some
form of readout e.g. by tagging with \ac{gfp}~\cite{Chalfie1994}.
In general, we can not assume that the model output and experimental results will match identically,
but rather be within some given margin of error.
We now consider the case where two cells $c_{m,1},c_{m,2}$ in the model output are approximately
equally close to the experimental state of a particular cell $c_e$ under this chosen metric, i.e.
$d(c_{m,1},c_e)\approx d(c_{m,2},c_e)$.
Without any additional input, we can not tell if $c_{m,1}$ or $c_{m,2}$ of the model output
corresponds to $c_e$ within the experiment.
Going one step further; even if the obtained distance values would exhibit a clear discrepancy, the
link between model and experimental reality could be flawed if simply the closest cell is also
assumed to be linked to the experimental one.
Such a situation can occur for example if the model output is far from the
actual biological reality.
Consider the case in which cell $c_{m,1}$ was first seeded with the information provided by
$c_e$ at the initial time point.
If the model produced results such that $c_{m,2}$ was closer to $c_e$,
the cells would be linked incorrectly by our previously envisioned metric and the resulting error
would not be caught.
This will not only result in a mismatched link of experiment and model but more importantly also
lead to a lower measured discrepancy between model and reality.
Such a behaviour is unacceptable when it comes to estimating parameters of any system and therefore
shows the clear need for another method of cellular identification.

\subsubsection{Cell Lineage}
In a final thought experiment we omit any spatial considerations or concepts of closeness.
Consider a system of two distinct cell species $A$ and $B$ where both types are able to divide but
in addition $A$ can differentiate into $B$.
The initial state consists of two cells $c_1\in A,c_2\in B$ of distinct species and the final state
of three cells $c_3\in A,c_4\in B,c_5\in B$.
The trajectories of this system are bound by the aforementioned dynamics.
We are therefore left with only two possible paths of achieving the final state which are depicted
in Figure~\ref{fig:theory-thought-experiment-2}.
Firstly, cell $c_2$ can divide into $c_4$ and $c_5$ while $c_1=c_3$ stays the same.
As an alternative $c_1$ can divide into $c_3$ and $c_4$ and the daughter cell $c_4$ also
differentiates into species $B$ while $c_2=c_5$ stays identical.
The initial and final state are identical with respect to cell count of the two species but the
biological interpretation differs substantially.
Process (A) is characterized by a division of species $B$ while (B) is characterized by a
division of species $A$ with differentiation.
Without knowing the de-facto parental relationship between cells $c_1,c_2$ and $c_3,c_4,c_5$, we can
give no further insights into the underlying dynamics thus missing out on this crucial biological
interpretation.

The preceding thought exercises would be resolved by assigning a unique identifier to each new
cellular entity which is not only unique at any given time point but throughout the whole
observed time interval.
This means in particular that even if a cell is removed from the simulation that no newcoming cell
will reuse any preexisting identifier.
Identifiers can not only be used to distinguish between agents but also to keep track of
parental relationships between the cells.
The idea of cellular identification being closely tied to their lineage is further made clear when
listing cellular relations as a family tree.
Figure~\ref{fig:theory-thought-experiment-3} shows a tree of multiple parents $p_i$, children
$c_j$ and grandchildren $gc_k$.
If we would perform a hypothetical exchange of child $c_{12}$ with $c_{21}$, this would mean that
two grandchild-cells $gc_{212}$ and $gc_{221}$ would have different grandparent-cells and thus
result in an invalid parental state.

\subsubsection{Definition of the Cellular State}
The considerations of the preceding section exemplify the need to uniquely label and track cells
throughout their life-time.
This can be achieved by assigning unique identifiers to each cell, which enables us to properly
trace them.

Every cell $c$ is a discrete entity that lives in a set $C$ which describes its possible
configurations.
We will therefore call this set the \textit{Cellular Configuration Space}.
It can describe properties such as the position of the cell, intracellular nutrient density or state
of the cell cycle.
For this particular example, the space would consist of a component for the position, i.e.
$\vec{x}\in\R^3$, nutrient density $\rho\in\R_{\geq 0}$ and cycle state which can be simplified for
an eukaryotic cell via $s\in\{1,2,3\}$ denoting resting, interphase and division phase
respectively~\cite{Alberts2018}.
In this example, the cellular configuration space is thus given by
$C=\R^3\times\R_{\geq 0}\times\{1,2,3\}$.

We supply this configuration space with an index set $I$ that holds all possible values of unique
identifiers which are assignable to cells.
We also keep a record of all identifiers that have already been used and store it in $I_p$.
Whenever a new cell is inserted, i.e. due to cell division, a corresponding new identifier $\iota$
is picked which is neither assigned to any currently living cell nor contained in $I_p$ and assigned
to this new cell.
Therefore, formally any cell is a tuple $(\iota,c)\in I\times C$ which we write as
$(\iota,c)=c_\iota$.
Furthermore, whenever a cell is removed, i.e. due to cell death, its identifier $\iota$ is stored
within $I_p$ which ensures that this cell is not assigned again to another cell.
This means that each cell $c\in C$ defined by its properties in the configuration space $C$ will
have a unique identifier $\iota\in I$ assigned to it.
Therefore, the collection of all cells at any time point is a subset of the power set
$M\subset Pot(I\times C)$.

\begin{definition}[Cellular State]
    \label{def:cellular-state}
    Let $I$ be a set of identifiers and $C$ be the cellular configuration space.
    We call a set of cells $(I_p,M)\in Pot(I)\times Pot(I\times C)$ a cellular state if
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Every cell has a unique identifer $\#\pi_1(M)=\#M$.
        \item Identifiers have not been used previously $I_p\cap\pi_1(M)=\emptyset$
    \end{enumerate}
    where $\pi$ is the projection on the first component.
    The set $I_p$ contains identifiers which have been used previously.
\end{definition}

We have so far not discussed how to construct the identifiers $\iota\in I$.
Their explicit choice can depend on the respective system in question and will be discussed later
(see \todo{ref}).

Definition~\ref{def:cellular-state} ensures that a cellular state is valid in the sense that
uniqueness of cells is ensured throughout their life-time.
However it does not allow us to describe any dynamical processes for the temporal evolution from one
cellular state into another.
Mapping one cellular state onto another, requires us to define the \textit{Cellular Space} on which
all dynamics are described as the collection of all cellular states.

\begin{definition}[Cellular Space]
    \label{def:cellular-space}
    The cellular space $\CS(I, C)$ of an index set $I$ and cellular configuration space $C$
    is given by
    \begin{equation}
        \CS(I,C) = \{M\in Pot(I)\times Pot(I\times C) | M \text{ is cellular state }\}.
    \end{equation}
    We choose a simplified notation $\CS = \CS(I, C)$ whenever the dependencies are clear from the
    given context.
    We also write $c_\iota:=(\iota,c)$ and introduce a simplified notation $c_\iota\in\CS$ where the
    index $\iota$ is taken with respect to all living cells $\iota\in \pi_1(M)$.
\end{definition}

We have now defined a structure which can capture snapshots of a variable amount of unique cellular
agents but are still missing any notion of dynamics.
While processes such as movement of the cells or physical interactions can change the individual
cellular configurations $c$, they preserve the cellular identity and thus the unique cells
$\pi_1(M)$ and $I_p$ which are contained within the state.

In general, it has to be noted that the explicit type of representation of the chosen identifiers
$I$ is irrelevant as long as they adhere to the discussed principles.
This leaves open various choices of how to define them which will be discussed later in
Example~\ref{example:unique-identifiers-1-global-counter} to
\ref{example:unique-identifiers-3-spatial-subdivision}.
Using any choice of such identifiers would circumvent the described problems of the previous
thought experiments and establish a direct link between cells in the model and
the experiment without relying on complex comparison metrics.

While these thoughts are puristic in nature, they set the stage for the comming theoretical
considerations.
Chapter~\ref{chapter:bacterial-rods} utilizes both properties; the uniqueness and
the parental relationships of the identifiers to construct metrics that can be used to estimate
parameters of a mechanical model for flexible elongated bacteria.
This shows that the presented ideas are not only of theoretical nature but can also be useful in
practical applications.

However, these considerations are bound by practical limitations and can be difficult
to practically realize in systems where high stochastic fluctuations lead to the decorrelation of
any two successively measured time points.
In such scenarios it is questionable if the concept of assigning unique identifiers to each cell
does provide meaningful insights into the dynamics of the system.

\subsection{Till Death or Division do us part}
To continue our investigation of uniqueness, we now want to turn our attention to processes such as
cell division and cell death that lead to changes in the cellular state which require updating the
tracked indices.
Definition~\ref{def:cellular-space} of the cellular space allows us to treat a variable number of
cells simultanously.
However, we have so far not discussed how one particular cellular state $(I_p,M)$ can be mapped onto
another $(J_p,N)$ by events such as cell division or death.

We first investigate different biological processes that correspond to distinct ways in which the
cardinality of the cellular state can be altered by a single division or death event.
These will shape the following definitions and guide the overall throught processes behind them.

\subsubsection{A Primer on Cellular Reproduction, Death \& Fusion}
On the subcellular level, these processes ocurr over extended periods of time.
In contrast, our \ac{abm} approach represents cells as discrete entities with well-defined division
and death timings which could be seen as one of its biggest conceptual weaknesses.

To further complicate matters, even the time point of division or death can sometimes not be
identified properly.
In animal cells the time of division can for instance be defined by the end of
cytokinesis~\cite{Gershony2014} which is the instant when the microtubule and membrane that form
the bridge between two daughter cells is cut.
However it was shown that human HeLa and mouse BALB/c-3T3 cells are already in $G_1$ state at this
time point~\cite{Gershony2014} meaning the cell cycle of the daughter cells has already started.
This emphasizes the point that division is a process on smaller time scales and not inherently
instantanous.
\acp{abm} usually operate on the mesoscopic scale in which it is the researcher's task to asses
which level of detail to utilize and which notion of division time is appropriate within the given
context.
Therefore, these processes should be captured by a corresponding dynamical description leading up to
the time point in question (see section~\ref{section:dynamics}) or be omitted if not required.

\paragraph{Division} via meiosis, mitosis or binary fission are among the most common types of
cellular reproduction~\cite{Biov2014,Ilowiecki1981,von1835resp}.
These process lead to the end of the mother's life and mark the beginning of the life the daughter
cells.

cells as displayed in Figure~\ref{fig:theory-division-basic}.
The mechanics of this process are assumed to alter the state of the mother cell in a significant
matter, which justifies the notion of two new daughter cells which are distinct entities.
However, this process does not need to guarantee that the resulting cells are exact copies but can
introduce variations and asymmetries.
Examples are the division of \ac{ecoli}~\cite{Sezonov2007} via binary fission, sporulation in yeasts
such as \textit{Schizosaccharomyces japonicus}~\cite{Neiman2011,Tanaka1982} and mitosis in Hek293
embryonic kidney cells~\cite{Molla2017}.
Internal budding is a process where to daughter buds form within a mother cell
and ultimately it before separating into two distinct cells.
It is observed in parasites such as \textit{Toxoplasma gondii}~\cite{Smyth1994} and can also be
described by the same mapping.
Notice that these examples include sexual and asexual reproduction.

\paragraph{Multiple fission} is another form of reproduction similar to the previous case but does
not produce two cells but rather a variable amount.
It can be found in some \textit{Myxogastria}~\cite{Baldauf1997}, in \textit{Blastocystis
hominis}~\cite{Suresh1994} and \textit{Trypanoplasma}~\cite{Peckov1990}.
For this less common case, Figure~\ref{fig:theory-division-basic} must be adjusted to produce an
arbitrary amount of cells $n$, not just $n=2$.

\paragraph{Multipolar budding} describes a process where multiple budding cites develop
simultanously at a single mother cell.
It has been observed in \textit{Paracoccidioides brasiliensis}~\cite{Almeida2009,Carbonell1967}
but can also be induced in budding yeast by increasing the abundance of polarity proteins within the
cell~\cite{Chiou2021} or by a complete rewiring of the feedback loop~\cite{Howell2009}.
In order to capture this phenomenon, we need to extend the right-hand side of
Figure~\ref{fig:theory-asymmetric-reproduction} from a single daughter cell $d$ to multiple daughter
cells $d_1,\dots,d_n$ in a similar fashion as done before.

\paragraph{Cellular Death} can ocurr in various ways such as apoptosis (programmed cell
death)~\cite{Kerr1972,BOHM2003,Green2010,Raff1998}, via autophagy~\cite{Licheva2025,Galluzzi2017} or
necrosis~\cite{Gerschenson2001,Fink2005} where the latter can also be regulated by genetic
pathways~\cite{Berghe2014}.
Similarly as before, cell-death is a process rather than a single event and these dynamics need to
be incorporated in the lead-up of the death event within the model.
Many cell types, even in tissues of high turnover rate, are known to release "Find-me" signals thus
requesting cleanup at a time point in which they are still fully intact~\cite{Ravichandran2011}.
Another mechanism is the release of growth-inducing substances that motivate neighboring cells to
compensate the loss of density~\cite{Fan2008}.
While mechanisms such as autophagy usually suppress apoptosis~\cite{Marshall2018,Boya2005}, an
extreme form of autophagy can trigger a type of cell death called autosis~\cite{Liu2013}.
These processes need to be accounted for in the dynamics of the respective system.
We are now again only interested in the instantanous time point at which the cellular entitiy, i.e.
agent is removed.
Figure~\ref{fig:theory-cell-death} shows the underwhelming process one a single cell that ceases to
exist.

\paragraph{Cellular fusion} is a process where multiple cells combine into one single new
cell~\cite{Ogle2004,Whitlock2024}.
Theodor Schwann hinted at this phenomenon as early as 1847~\cite{Schwann1847} but it would take
until 1960 before it was first achieved experimentally~\cite{Barski1960}.
Its arguably most notable example is the fertilization process of a human oocyte which is the fusion
of the sperm and egg into a single-celled zygote~\cite{Cobb2012,Neaves2017,Primakoff2002}.
Fusion can also be observed in the formation of skeletal muscle fibers which was initially believed
to be an artifact of lab-grown cells but was later replicated \textit{in Vivo}~\cite{Mintz1967}.
Using the \textit{Drosophila} model orgnaism, it could be shown that myblast fusion is asymmetrical
and ocurrs between distinct classes of cells where one cell acts as a "Founder cell"
while the remaining partners mainly contribute bulk mass~\cite{Bate1990,Bour2000}.
Another example is the mammalian placenta whose outer tissue layer consists of
\textit{syncytiotrophoblast} with multiple nuclei~\cite{Midgley1963}.
Surprisingly, it was found that the \textit{Syncytin-1} protein driving the fusion is not a human
gene but rather of a virus whose genetic code is now being repurposed~\cite{Mi2000,Dupressoir2009}.

The listed examples show in great detail that the underlying complexity warrants that the resulting
cell can both be treated as a new cell (e.g. fertilization) or as an existing cell (e.g. myoblast
fusion).
Fusing two cells can therefore be viewed as either removing both cells and inserting a completely
new one or removing one cell and adjusting the state of the other.
This process is displayed schematically in Figure~\ref{fig:theory-cell-fusion}.

\paragraph{Other forms of insertion and removal} need to be accounted for as well.
When modeling cellular systems, most computational studies are only able to capture a small slice of
the actual size of the biological reality present.
Within this scope cells can be added or removed to the observable environment simply by moving
into or out of the observed physical region.
This can be seen in a puristic manner in microfluidic devices such as mother
machines~\cite{Wang2010Robust} in which bacteria can be kept in a state of continuous
growth~\cite{Yang2018,Kaiser2018} while excess cells are sucked away by fluidic
forces~\cite{Grnberger2014}.
However, when studying other systems such as the varying modes of cancer migration~\cite{Friedl2003}
or swarming of \textit{Paenibacillus}~\cite{Ingham2008}, this puristic notion can not be upheld and
tracability of cells begins to get messy.

One common technique to avoid the challenges of inspecting a subslice of a larger biological system
is to consider periodic spatial boundary conditions within the constructed model.
This is for instance commonly employed in densely packed epithelial cell
sheets~\cite{Bi2015,Bi2016,Tong2022} but can only be applied when a sufficiently small area of the
complete tissue is investigated and is only a valid simplification in certain justified scenarios
and not generally applicable across all systems.

Another case that deliberate cellular insertion is of practical importance can be made when
investigating the effect of predator-like cells such as \textit{Bdellovibrio bacteriovorus} on the
growth of \ac{ecoli}~\cite{Sockett2009}.
In these studies, preformed prey assays are used where the \ac{ecoli} colony is allowed to grow,
often by forming biofilms, before the predator is introduced into the system~\cite{Kadouri2005}.

\subsubsection{Formalizing Cellular Reproductive \& Death Processes}
We now turn our attention to the question of how to mathemtically capture the previously discussed
cellular processes.
Each of the presented examples can be viewed as a combination of removal of a cell, insertion of
new cells and modification of existing ones which all depend on the current state of the related
cells (mother cell or constituent cells for fusion).
We first focus on the reproductive processes and cell death and will discuss cellular fusion with
other forms of cellular insertion and removal later.

Division such as binary fission or mitosis can be viewed as the removal of the mother
cell and an insertion of two new daughter cells which can be captured in a mapping of the form
$C\rightarrow C\times C$.
Extending this simple map to also apply to multiple fission will yield a tuple of cells
$(c_1,c_2,\dots)$ which live in a product of the form $C^n$ where $n\geq 2$.
However, the amount of newly created cells $n$ is variable and therefore the extended map takes
values in the disjoint union $\sqcup_{n\geq 2}C^n$.
The disjoint union ensures that the mapping takes values in exactly one of the $C^n$ and can be
formalized via $\sqcup_{n\geq 2}C^n = \cup_{n\geq 2}(n,C^n)$.

As explained earlier, other processes such as budding will not remove the mother cell but instead
modify it and additionally insert a new daughter cell at the division time point.
In its most simple form this can also be captured by another map $C\rightarrow C\times C$ where
the interpretation is now distinct to the previous one.
We define the convention that the first component (cell) of the map is the mother cell which is
modified while the second component (cell) is the new daughter cell (compare with
Figure~\ref{fig:theory-asymmetric-reproduction}) which is inserted.
Extending this asymmetric division to include multipolar budding yet again takes values in the
disjoint union of all $C^n$ where the convention that the first cell is the modified mother cell
still holds.

In order to distinguish between the two cases, we additionally produce a boolean value $s=0,1$
which indicates if the mother cell is removed $s=0$ or kept $s=1$.
The final result is a division map of the form
\begin{equation}
    \gamma:C\rightarrow\{0,1\} \times \sqcup_{n\geq 2}C^n
\end{equation}
with two distinct interpretations that need to be taken into account when mapping one cellular
state $(I_p,M)$ onto another.
In order to apply the single-division function to a particular cell, we introduce an operator
$\Upsilon$ which utilizes the division function $\gamma$ and applies it to a particular cell
$c_\iota$ defined by the identifier $\iota\in M$.

The cell-specific functionality is encoded within $\gamma$ and thus $\Upsilon$ should solely
manage administrative tasks such as the creation and removal of unique new identifiers of the cells.
Therefore, $\Upsilon$ can be defined abstractly without the particular knowledge of $\gamma$ and is
specific to the respective simulation framework rather than to the model itself.
It will depend on the choice of identifiers $\iota\in I$ but the overall dynamics of the system
must be invariant under the particular choice of utilized identifiers $I$.

We will now introduce a convention that simplifies the notation of division functions.
Most common examples will deal with only one form of binary division such as binary fission, mitosis
or budding.
This means, that the most common division function will be of the form
$\gamma:C\rightarrow C\times C$ where the indicator $\gamma(c)_0$ of the mother cells removal or
persistance is fixed, meaning $\gamma(c)_0=s\forall c$.
This allows us to factorize $\gamma$ via
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tikzpicture}
        \node (i1) at (0,0) {$C$};
        \node (i2) at (3,0) {$\{0,1\}\times\sqcup_{n\geq 2}C^n$};
        \node (i3) at (0,-1.5) {$C\times C$};
        \draw[->] (i1) -- node[above] {$\tilde{\gamma}$} ++ (i2);
        \draw[->] (i1) -- node[left] {$\gamma$} ++ (i3);
        \draw[->] (i2) -- node[right,below,xshift=1em] {$\pi_{2,3}$} ++ (i3);
    \end{tikzpicture}
\end{figure}
where $\pi_{2,3}$ is the projection onto the components $(2,3)$ and
$\tilde{\gamma}(c)_0=\gamma(c)=s$.
This means whenever a division function $\gamma:C\rightarrow C\times C$ is presented, we will be
able to utilize it by applying $\tilde{\gamma}$ within the respective simulation framework.

\begin{definition}[Single Cell Division]
    We call a map $\gamma:C\rightarrow \{0,1\}\times\sqcup_{n\geq 2}C^n$ a division function
    with output $\gamma(c_\iota) = (s,(c^1,\dots,c^n))$ where we call the variable $s$ the (binary)
    \textbf{Mode of Division}.
    This definition formalizes criteria under which the map
    \begin{equation}
        \Upsilon:\{\gamma:C\rightarrow
            \{0,1\}\times\sqcup_{n\geq 2}C^n\}\times I\times\CS\rightarrow\CS
    \end{equation}
    is called a \textbf{Division Operator}.
    We call its input $(I_p,M)\in\CS$ the initial state with $c_\iota\in M$ indicating the mother
    cell and $(J_p,N)=\Upsilon(\gamma,\iota,(I_p,M))$ the final state.
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item (Removal) Exactly one cell is removed $M\backslash N=\{c_\iota\}$ if
            the mother cell is replaced $\gamma(c_\iota)_0=0$ during the division.
            Otherwise no cell is removed $M\backslash N=\emptyset$.
        \item (Insertion) The inserted cells are given by
            \begin{equation}
                N\backslash M = \{c_{\iota_{1+s}},\dots,c_{\iota_n}\}
            \end{equation}
            where the new cells $c_{\iota_j}=c^j$ match the output of $\gamma(c_\iota)_1$.
        \item (Update)
            If the mother cell is not removed, i.e. $s=1$, we require
            $N\ni c_\iota=c^1$, i.e. the mother cell identified by identifier $\iota$ is updated
            with the value of the first component of $\gamma(c_\iota)_1$.
            Notice that this assignment of a new value does not change the identifier $\iota$ of the
            mother cell.
        \item (Tracking) We carry forward all old identifiers and the removed one
            $J_p=I_p\cup\{\iota\}$ depending on the mode $s=0$ or otherwise require $J_p:=I_p$.
            % \todo[inline]{ensure that all identifiers are still related to the same cells as before}
        \item (Uniqueness) New cells have new identifiers: $\iota_{1+s},\dots,\iota_n\notin J_p$.
    \end{enumerate}
    In the case when $i\notin\pi_1(M)$, we require $\Upsilon(\gamma,i,(I_p,M))=(I_p,M)$.
    \label{definition:cell-division-single}
\end{definition}

It is important to note that, while this definition seems cumbersome in the first place, it is
essential in maintaining a valid cellular state.
The last two conditions of Definition~\ref{definition:cell-division-single} are to be thought of as
one in order to guarantee uniqueness for each cell over the course of the total time of our system.
Note also that the combination of property (4) and (5) ensures that the identifier $\iota$ of the
mother cell is not reused for any of the daughter cells.
The following corollary is a direct consequence of our definition.

\begin{corollary}
    Any living cell not targeted by the division operation persists in its original state which is
    a direct consequence of property (2) in Definition~\ref{definition:cell-division-single}.
\end{corollary}

So far, we have only applied the division function $\gamma$ to a single cell of interest $c_\iota$.
In order to apply multiple division operators successively, we first state two observations that
aim to provide a clear picture of the coming definitions.

\begin{lemma}
    For any identifiers $\kappa\in K\subset\pi_1(M)$ of living cells, the successive application of
    the division operator $\Upsilon$ with possibly varying division functions $\gamma_\kappa$ can
    only lead to differences in the identifiers of the daughter cells.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    This is a consequence of the preceding corollary and the fact that identifiers of mother cells
    either get removed or persist.
    Since the identifiers of mother cells $\kappa\in K$ will be removed, the resulting sets of
    previously assigned identifiers $J_p$ will be identical.
    In the case where two orders of divisions $\kappa_{i_l},\kappa_{j_l}$ also lead to the same set
    of new identifiers
    \begin{align}\begin{split}
        (J_p,L_i) &= \Upsilon(\gamma_{\kappa_{i_0}},\kappa_{i_0}, \Upsilon(\gamma_{\kappa_{i_1}},k_i,\dots))\\
        (J_p,L_j) &= \Upsilon(\gamma_{\kappa_{j_0}},\kappa_{j_0}, \Upsilon(\gamma_{\kappa_{j_1}},k_j,\dots))\\
        L_i &= L_j
        \label{eq:theory-reorder-division-operators}
    \end{split}\end{align}
    they can be mapped onto each other via an isomorphism between two sets of identical cardinality.
    However Definition~\ref{definition:cell-division-single} does not generally require that the
    reordering property of Equation~\ref{eq:theory-reorder-division-operators} holds true in all
    cases.
\end{proof}

With these two statements, we have shown what we have already previously discussed.
The unique identifiers $\iota$ which are assigned to each individual cell do not carry any
particular meaning but rather relate them to their parent and are used to track them throughout
their lifetime and distinguish them from other cells.
For this reason, it is irrelevant in which order multiple division operations at the same time
point are treated and it is left for to the respective simulation framework to choose.

However, one important question that remains is when a division is triggered.
So far we have not delved deeper into how to define the dynamics of the \ac{abm}.
While cells interact with their environment and other cells at any given moment, the time point of
division is purely determined by their internal state as in the previously discussed example of
the end of cytokinesis~\cite{Gershony2014}.
This allows us to define a division criterion $\bar{\gamma}:C\rightarrow\{0,1\}$ which can be
evaluated for any state of the cell and will determine if the cell should divide, i.e.
$\bar{\gamma}(c)=1$ or not $\bar{\gamma}(c)=0$.

\begin{definition}[Cell Division Criterion]
    A division criterion is a map $\bar{\gamma}:C\rightarrow\{0,1\}$ which determines if the
    single division function of Definition~\ref{definition:cell-division-single} for the particular
    cell $c$ is realized.
    \label{definition:cell-division-criterion}
\end{definition}

Another important process is cell death which ocurrs over a time span, similarly to cell division.
As discussed before, cell-death comes in various forms which can lead to differences in interactions
of the dying cells with their environment.
The dynamics of the processes leading up to the death event such as cell-cell signalling need to be
accounted for within the dynamics of the model (see Section~\ref{section:dynamics}).
We again now focus on the time point at which the cellular entitiy is considered to no longer exist
and therefore removed from the model.
Later we will slightly extend this definition in order to account for processes that ocurr right at
the time of death such as the release of intracellular compounds~\cite{Kono2008,Chen2010}.
This requires us to introduce the notion of the environment of the cell which is able to take up the
released compounds.

As discussed earlier, the removal of a cell does not have to be the result of a death event but
could also be due to the cell leaving the inspected spatial region (subslice) of interest which is
common in mother machines.
Definition~\ref{definition:cell-removal} captures the instant at which the cellular entitiy is
removed.

\begin{definition}[Cell Removal]
    % We call a map $\zeta:I_p\rightarrow \text{Pot}(I_p)$ a death-function
    A map $\delta:I\times\CS\rightarrow\CS$ is called a removal function if for $\iota\in\pi_1(M)$
    and $\delta(\iota,(I_p,M))=(J_p,N)$, the two conditions
    \begin{align}\begin{split}
        I_p\cup\{\iota\}&=J_p\\
        \pi_1(M\backslash N)&=\{c_\iota\}
    \end{split}\end{align}
    hold where $\pi_1$ is the projection onto the first component.
    In particular if $\iota\notin\pi_1(M)$, then $I_p=J_P$ and $M=N$.
    \label{definition:cell-removal}
\end{definition}

Removal of cells is also indicated purely by their internal state and can be formalized with a
criterion similarly to the division criterion of
Definition~\ref{definition:cell-division-criterion}.

\begin{definition}[Cell Removal Criterion]
    A removal criterion is a map $\bar{\delta}:C\rightarrow\{0,1\}$ which determines if the cell in
    question is removed by the removal function of Definition~\ref{definition:cell-removal}.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Cell Lineage]
    Given an initial state $(I_p,M)\in\CS$, an arbitrary combination of applications of
    division and death functions creates a forest.
    \label{lemma:cell-lineage}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    We proof this statement via induction. For $n=0$ we are presented with a cellular state
    $M\in\CS$ which consists of cells $\{c_\iota\in M\}$.
    We interpret each cell as root of a new tree.
    In the induction step $n\rightarrow n+1$, we may assume that $G=(C,E)$ is a tree with
    cells as vertices.
    Any application of a division function $\Upsilon(\gamma, \dot)$ consumes a cell $c_{\iota_0}$
    and produces new cells $\{c_{\iota_1},\dots,c_{\iota_n}\}$.
    We thus insert the new cells as additional vertices with the edges
    $\{c_{\iota_0},c_{\iota_1}\},\dots,\{c_{\iota_0},c_{\iota_n}\}$.
    From the Uniqueness property of the cell-division
    definition~\ref{definition:cell-division-single},
    we know that the index of the removed cell $c_{\iota_0}$ will not occur for any further
    application of the division operator which means that the vertex of cell $c_{\iota_0}$ has $n$
    children.
    In the case of a death function, nothing happens.
    The associated cell $c_\iota$ will remain and endpoint for any further applications of death
    or division.
\end{proof}
\begin{corollary}[$k$-ary and Binary Cell Division]
    In the case where the single division function
    $\eta:C\rightarrow C^k\subset\cup_{n\in\mathbb{N}}C^n$
    is of k-ary nature, lemma~\ref{lemma:cell-lineage} retains a k-ary tree.
    The most common biological reality is the binary case.
\end{corollary}

\section{Dynamics}
\begin{definition}[Dynamical System]
    A dynamical system is a tuple $(T,M,\phi)$ where $T$ is a monoid (written additively) and
    $\phi$ is a mapping
    \begin{equation}
        \phi : U\subset(T\times M) \rightarrow M
    \end{equation}
    with $\pi_2(U) = M$ where $\pi_2$ is the projection on the second coordinate and $\phi$
    satisfies
    \begin{align}
        \phi(0,m) &= m\\
        \phi(t_2, \phi(t_1, m)) &= \phi(t_2+t_1,m).
    \end{align}
\end{definition}

We often write $\phi(t,m) = \phi_t(m)$.
The dynamics of \acp{abm} are governed by agents and their interactions with each other and the
environment.
Thus we define our dynamical system to be of this shape.

\begin{definition}
    An \ac{abm} is a dynamical system which consists of a Cellular Space $\CS$ and an environment
    $\EV$.
    \begin{equation}
        M = \CS\times\EV
    \end{equation}
    Given an initial state $x_0$, we say that a cell $c$ with index $\iota$ is alive at point $t$ if
    $c_\iota\in\pi_1(\phi_t(x))$.
\end{definition}

It is of importance to consider the environment as an integral part of the
dynamical system such that
interactions of the agents with it can permanently alter its constitution.
In this way, extracellular constituents can interact and change and even the overall spatial
configuration of the environment could be subject to dynamics.
Furthermore, we do not require that the monoid $T$ is a subset of the real numbers in order to allow
for discrete dynamical systems.
Furthermore, for simplicity we omit any Stochasticity from our system \todo{Can we fix this?}.
Practical applications which implement numerical routines should rely on pseudo-random number
generators which will produce deterministic results when given an initial seed.
By treating this initial seed as part of the initial values of our system, we are able to describe
pseudo-stochastic processes within a deterministic framework.

\begin{lemma}[Lifetime of a cell]
    \label{thm:cellular-uniqueness}
    Given an index $\iota\in I$ to an initial state $x$ of an \ac{abm}, we define the
    \textbf{Life-Span} of $\iota$ which is the set of all time-points for which the cell is alive
    \begin{equation}
        T_\iota=\{t\in T: c_\iota \text{ is alive}\}.
    \end{equation}
    If $T$ is an ordered and path-connected topological space, then $T_\iota$ is also
    path-connected.
    If $\mu$ is a measure on $T$, we also define the \textbf{Lifetime} with respect to this measure
    via
    \begin{equation}
        t_\iota:=\mu\left(T_\iota\right).
    \end{equation}
    In practial examples, $\mu$ will be given by the Lebesgue measure.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    Let $t<s\in T_\iota$ arbitrary.
    We pick a path $\gamma:[0,1]\rightarrow T$ from $t$ to $s$.
    Since $T$ is ordered, we can pick a path which satisfies $t\leq\gamma(h)\leq s$.
    By Definition~\ref{definition:cell-division-single}, properties 1 and 4, we see
    that every state
    between the points of insertion and removal are part of $T_\iota$ and thus
    $\gamma(h)\in T_\iota\forall h\in [0,1]$.
\end{proof}

\subsubsection{Relation to Identifiability Analysis}
Parameter estimation usually uses weighted sum of residuals
\begin{equation}
    \chi^2_\text{res}(p) = \sum\limits_{k=1}^m\sum\limits_{l=1}^{d_k}\left(\frac{y_{kl}^D
    - g_k(p,t_l)}{\sigma^D_{kl}}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{kl}^D$ is the measurement error for datapoint $y_{kl}^D$ and time point $t_l$.
The most common estimator is the maximum likelihood estimator
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{p} = \text{arg min}\left[\chi^2_\text{res}(p)\right].
\end{equation}

\begin{definition}[Structural Identifiability]
    A model is \textit{structurally identifiable} if a unique parametrization exists for
    any given model output.
    A parameter $p_i$ is called \textit{globally structurally identifiable} if for every
    parameter vector $\textbf{p}$, it holds
    \begin{equation}
        y(p)=y(p') \Rightarrow p_i=p_i'.
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

\subsection{Coarse-graining of an off-lattice center-based model}
\subsubsection{Motivation}
This section formulates a generalized model for cellular dynamics, covering growth, division,
intracellular \& extracellular reactions and mechanical properties.
We assume that each cell is represented by a center-based, off-lattice point
$\textbf{x}_c\in\R^d$ and interacts in a
point-wise manner with the external environment.
This approach is general enough to cover a wide range of cellular systems while also providing the
necessary assumptions to derive meaningful results.
We model the system over the course of a time interval $I\subset\R$.
Within the model every alive cell $c\in\CS$ is described by an individual set of
equations for volume $V_c$, intracellular reactions $\textbf{v}_c$ and mechanics $\textbf{x}_c$.
These equations can be subject to heterogeneity and are thus not assumed to be identical in their
most generalized version.

\subsubsection{Case 1: Full Reduction}
We consider the case with no cellular growth $h_{g,c}=0$, and aim to exclude any spatial
dependence by integrating over the domain $\EV$.
Furthermore, we assume that the dimensionality of intra- and extracellular reactions is
identical $n=m$ and that the exchange term is given by a fast linear term
$\textbf{h}_x~(\textbf{v}_c - \textbf{w})$.
These choices reduce the auxiliary matrices to $\textbf{T}_i=\textbf{T}_e=\mathbb{1}$.
Under the assumption that the exchange is fast compared the remaining processes, we can
assume that $\textbf{v}_c=\textbf{w}|_{\textbf{x}_c}$.
This considerably simplifies our system of equations leaving us with
\begin{equation}
    \partial_t\textbf{w} = \sum\limits_c
    h_{i,c}(\textbf{w}|_{\textbf{x}_c})\frac{V_c}{V_\EV}\delta(\textbf{x}-\textbf{x}_c) +
    \textbf{h}_e(\textbf{w}) + \textbf{S}(\textbf{w}).
\end{equation}
We integrate over the domain $\EV$ and obtain use the total amount of extracellular
compounds $\bar{\textbf{w}}:I\rightarrow\R^n$ instead of the previously used
concentrations $\textbf{w}$ to reformulate the resulting equation.
Carrying out these steps leads to
\begin{alignat}{7}
    \partial_t\bar{\textbf{w}} &=
    &\int\limits_\EV\sum\limits_c&
    \textbf{h}_{i,c}(\textbf{w})\frac{V_c}{V_\EV}\delta(\textbf{x} - \textbf{x}_c)\text{dV}
    &+& \bar{\textbf{h}}_e(\bar{\textbf{w}})
    &+& \int\limits_\EV\textbf{S}(\textbf{w})\text{dV}\\
    &= &\sum\limits_c& V_c\textbf{h}_{i,c}(\bar{\textbf{w}}) &+&
    \bar{\textbf{h}}_e(\bar{\textbf{w}})
    \label{eq:pool-model-case1-coarse-grained-result}
\end{alignat}
which is the direct combination of intra- and extracellular reactions.
Note that the final integral vanishes due to our assumptions that
$\textbf{S}(\textbf{w})$ only describes transport terms, leaving the total amount invariant.
The first term can be understood in terms of an expectation value $\text{E}(X_c)=\sum_c
X_c / N_\CS$ of a product of two distributions over living cells $c\in\CS$ which we will
use to rewrite equation~\eqref{eq:pool-model-case1-coarse-grained-result}.
In the general case where the distributions $V_c/V_\EV$ and
$\bar{\textbf{h}}_{i,c}(\bar{\textbf{w}})$ are not independent, we obtain
\begin{equation}
    \partial_t\bar{\textbf{w}} =
    N_\CS\text{E}\left(V_c\right)\text{E}(\textbf{h}_{i,c}({\bar{\textbf{w}}}))
    + N_\CS\text{Cov}\left(V_c,\textbf{h}_{i,c}({\bar{\textbf{w}}})\right)
    + \bar{\textbf{h}}_e(\bar{\textbf{w}})
\end{equation}

which can be further reduced, assuming that the distribution of volumes $V_c$ and
intracellular reactions is stochastically independent:
\begin{alignat}{5}
    \partial_t\bar{\textbf{w}} &=
    &N_\CS\text{E}\left(V_c\right)&\text{E}(\textbf{h}_{i,c}({\bar{\textbf{w}}}))
    &+& \bar{\textbf{h}}_e(\bar{\textbf{w}})\\
    &= &q& \textbf{h}_i(\textbf{w}) &+& \bar{\textbf{h}}_e(\bar{\textbf{w}})
\end{alignat}
We have used the expectation value to factorize the first term and $q=N_\CS\text{E}(V_c)$
with $\textbf{h}_i=\text{E}(\textbf{h}_{i,c})$ to simplify notation.
Using this concise notation, the final step shows that our equations reduce to a set of \acp{ode}.
The value $q$ is the fraction of total bacterial volume compared to the volume of the
domain $V_\EV$.

We can also reinterpret the sum in
equation~\eqref{eq:pool-model-case1-coarse-grained-result} via a continuous measure using
the probability to encounter a certain number of cells in the state $c\in C$.
For this reason it is desirable to omit the cellular identity at this point, thus
introducing the cellular density as a function $\rho:C\rightarrow\R$.
In the continuum limit, the sum transitions into an integral over the cellular
configuration space $C$ (see Section~\ref{subsec:cellular-space})
\begin{equation}
    \partial_t\bar{\textbf{w}}
    = \int\limits_C N_\CS\rho(c)\textbf{h}_{i,c}(\bar{\textbf{w}})\text{d}c
    + \bar{\textbf{h}}_e(\bar{\textbf{w}}).
\end{equation}

We can therefore interpret $\textbf{h}_{i,c}$ as a distribution of reaction functions and
the integral

\begin{equation}
    F[h_c] = \int\limits_C N_\CS\rho(c)h_c\text{d}c
\end{equation}

as an operator acting on this space.

\subsubsection{Case 4: Something Else}
Although the equations governing the \ac{abm} can not be derived directly from a higher
conceptual viewpoint, we are still able to motivate them and explain their individual components.
To this end, we consider the total energy stored in the system to be constant.
It is made up of the environment's energy and the energy stored within the cells, either
in the form of intracellular nutrients or converted into biomass.
This means that no additional nutrients are added and the existing energy is only
converted internally (i.e. from diffusive nutrients into cellular mass).
We denote the density of extracellular nutrients with $R_e$ and the intracellular
nutrients of species $A/B$ with $R_{i,c_{A/B}}$ respectively.
By integrating over the respective volumes and summing over all cellular agents, we
obtain the total amount of energy.
\begin{alignat}{5}
    \text{const}
    &=\int\limits_{V_D} R_e(x) \text{dVol} &+ \sum\limits_c\int\limits_{V_c}&R_{i,c}\text{dVol}\\
    &=\int\limits_{V_D} R_e(x) \text{dVol} &+ \sum\limits_c&R_{i,c}V_c
    \label{eq:supplement-abm-derivation-enthalpy-const}
\end{alignat}
We treat intracellular concentrations of nutrients $R_{i,c}$ as spatially homogenous
within each cell.
The resulting quantity can also be interpreted as the thermodynamic Enthalpy of the system.
It is usually defined as the product of pressure and volume but can expressed in terms of
the density when employing a linear equation of state relating pressure and density.
We postulate that this quantity stays constant.
From equation~\ref{eq:supplement-abm-derivation-enthalpy-const}, we can take the
derivative with respect to time and obtain
\begin{equation}
    0 =
    \int\limits_{V_D}\dot{R}_e(x)\text{dVol}
    + \sum\limits_c \left(\dot{R}_{i,c}V_c + R_{i,c}\dot{V}_c\right).
    \label{eq:supplement-abm-derivation-enthalpy-derivative}
\end{equation}
In order to link the extracellular nutrients $R_e$ to the intracellular ones $R_{i,c}$,
we add osmotic exchange terms of the forms
\begin{equation}
    \pm a
    \sum\limits_c\int\limits_{V_c}\frac{V_c}{V_D}\delta(x-x_c)(R_e(x)-R_{i,c})\text{dVol}
    = \pm a\sum\limits_c V_c (R_e(x_c)-R_{i,c})
    \label{eq:supplement-abm-derivation-osmotic-exchange-2}
\end{equation}
which are proportional to the difference of intra- to extracellular nutrient density.
In total we can now rewrite equation~\ref{eq:supplement-abm-derivation-enthalpy-derivative} to be
\begin{alignat}{7}
    0 &=
    \int\limits_{V_D}\dot{R}_e(x)\text{dVol}
    &-a\sum\limits_{c}&\int\limits_{V_{c}}&\frac{V_{c}}{V_D}&(R_e(x)-R_{i,c})\delta(x-x_{c})\text{dVol}&&
    \label{eq:supplement-abm-derivation-reactions-1}\\
    &&+ \sum\limits_{c}\Big[& &a&(R_e(x_{c})-R_{i,c}) V_{c} &+& \dot{R}_{i,c}V_{c} +
    R_{i,c}\dot{V}_{c}\Big]
    \label{eq:supplement-abm-derivation-reactions-2}
\end{alignat}
Upon closer inspection, we can now appreciate that the individual components of this
equation can be understood as spatially distributed processes (equation
\ref{eq:supplement-abm-derivation-reactions-1}) and intracellular processes respective to
individual cells \ref{eq:supplement-abm-derivation-reactions-2}.
We can ensure the conservation of energy be setting all of these components to zero
individually and requiring equality under the integral.
Additionally dividing by the volume $V_c$ of the cells for terms
\ref{eq:supplement-abm-derivation-reactions-2} and introducing diffusion for the
extracellular nutrients $R_e$ leads to
\begin{alignat}{3}
    \dot{R}_e(x) &=&-a\sum\limits_{c}\frac{V_{c}}{V_D}&(R_e(x)-R_{i,c})\delta(x-x_{c}) +
    D\Delta R_e(x)\\
    \dot{R}_{i,c} &= &+ a &(R_e(x_{c}) - R_{i,c_B}) -R_{i,c}\frac{\dot{V}_{c}}{V_{c}}
    \label{eq:supplement-abm-derivation-reactions-fin-2}
\end{alignat}
For the diffusive term $D\Delta R_e$, we assume Neumann boundary conditions such that no
extracellular nutrients $R_e$ are lost or gained via this process.
\begin{equation}
    \int\limits_D \Delta R_e(x) \text{dVol} = \int\limits_{\partial D} \nabla
    R_e(x)\text{d\textbf{A}}= 0
\end{equation}
It should be noted in particular that the terms
\ref{eq:supplement-abm-derivation-reactions-fin-2} are maintained within each individual
cell, not just on a global level.
Thus the system now consists of $1+N_A+N_B$ equations in total where $N_{A/B}$ are the
number of cells of species $A/B$ which changes (i.e. increases) over the course of the simulation.
We denote this fact and in general cell-specific properties with indices $c$ or $c_{A/B}$
if species-specific properties are at play.
In addition, we pick a growth model depending on the species $A/B$ of our cell
\begin{alignat}{3}
    \dot{V}_{c_A} &= &u \sigma &R_{i,c_A} V_{c_A}
    \label{eq:supplement-abm-derivation-growth-model-1}\\
    \dot{V}_{c_B} &= &\frac{u}{1+\nu_I I(x_{c_B})} \sigma &R_{i,c_B} V_{c_B}
    \label{eq:supplement-abm-derivation-growth-model-2}
\end{alignat}
which is proportional to the total cellular nutrients given by the product of the
intracellular nutrient density $R_{i,c}$ and its volume $V_c$.
Equation \ref{eq:supplement-abm-derivation-growth-model-2} is modulated by the abundance
of an extracellular inhibitor resource $I(x)$ whose dynamics are not yet specified.
However, these dynamics do not play any role for our the conservation law as it only
controls the rate of the growth.
The two factors $a,\sigma$ represent the rate with which the intracellular nutrients are
depleted and the conversion factor which turns nutrients into cellular volume respectively.

\subsubsection{Case 5: Pool Model Considerations}
\label{sec:supplement-abm-derivation}
The equations governing the dynamics of the \ac{abm} can not be directly derived from
higher principles but can still aim to motivate them as best as possible.
Our model consists of individual cells with volume $V_c$ and extracellular nutrients
$R_e(x)$ which are spatially distributed.
We set up a balancing equation that aims to relate these concepts.
To this end, we assume that any loss in extracellular nutrients is directly converted
into cellular volume.
To compare any changes in $R_e$ with changes of volume, we introduce a conversion factor $\sigma$.
The balancing equation then reads
\begin{equation}
    \text{const} = \sigma \int\limits_D R_e(x)\text{dVol} + \sum\limits_c V_c.
\end{equation}
We now assume that there are two species $A,B$ present and split the sum and since we are
interested in the dynamics of the system, we also take the temporal derivative.
By adding a zero in the form of
\begin{equation}
    \pm u_c\sigma \sum\limits_c\int\limits_D\frac{V_c}{V_D}R_e(x)\delta(x-x_c)\text{dVol}
\end{equation}
we can introduce an exchange term between extracellular nutrients and cellular volume.
In total we obtain
\begin{alignat}{3}
    0 &= \sigma\int\limits_D\dot{R}_e(x)\text{dVol} &+ \sigma\sum\limits_c
    u_c&\int\limits_D\frac{V_c}{V_D}R_e(x)\delta(x-x_c)\text{dVol}\\
    &&- \sigma\sum\limits_c
    u_c&\int\limits_D\frac{V_c}{V_D}R_e(x)\delta(x-x_c)\text{dVol} + \sum\limits_c \dot{V}_c\\
    &= \sigma\int\limits_D\Big[\dot{R}_e(x) &+ \sum\limits_c
    u_c&\frac{V_c}{V_D}R_e(x)\delta(x-x_c)\Big]\text{dVol}
    \label{eq:supplement-abm-derivation-intermediate-3}\\
    &&- \sum\limits_c\Big[\sigma u_c&V_cR_e(x_c) + \dot{V}_c\Big].
    \label{eq:supplement-abm-derivation-intermediate-4}
\end{alignat}
In order to satisfy this balancing equation, we can enforce equality of the dynamics for
the subsystems given by \ref{eq:supplement-abm-derivation-intermediate-3} and
\ref{eq:supplement-abm-derivation-intermediate-4}.
These can be viewed as the extracellular dynamics surrounding the nutrient resource $R_e$
and intracellular dynamics increasing the volume $V_c$.
We assume that the spatial distribution of $R_e$ is governed by a diffusion process with
Neumann boundary conditions such that no additional nutrients are introduced and no nutrients lost.
In particular, any diffusion effects vanishe in the integral
\begin{equation}
    \int\limits_D\Delta R_e(x)\text{dVol} = \int\limits_{\partial D}\nabla
    R_e(x)\text{d\textbf{A}} = 0
\end{equation}
due to the Neumann boundary conditions.
We separate the preceding equations and enforce equality of the dynamics under the integral.
We therefore obtain
\begin{align}
    \dot{R}_e(x) &= -\sigma\sum\limits_c u_c\frac{V_c}{V_D}R_e(x)\delta(x-x_c) + D_R\Delta R_e(x)\\
    \dot{V}_c &= \sigma u_c R_e(x_c)V_c
\end{align}
where $D_R$ is the diffusion constant of the nutrients $R_e$.
So far, we have treated all cells equally but the system which we aim to describe
consists of two distinct species $A/B$ which behave differently.
We can express this variation by introducing differing growth terms $u_c$ depending on the species.
This splits apart the sum, raning over cells $c_A$ and $c_B$.
Another mechanism of this system is the inhibition of growth mediated by an external
inhibitor $I$ which is produced by species $A$.
We assume that the release of inhibitor molecules can be neglected for the assumptions
governing the initial balancing of the system.
In addition to the production of the inhibitor, we also introduce diffusion with constant
$D_I$ which results in
\begin{equation}
    \dot{I}(x) = b\sum\limits_{c_A}\frac{V_{c_A}}{V_D}\delta(x-x_{c_A}) + D_I \Delta I(x).
\end{equation}
By splitting up all terms with respect to species, we arrive at our final equations
\begin{alignat}{5}
    \dot{V}_{c_A} &= &\sigma u& R_e(x_c)V_c\\
    \dot{V}_{c_B} &= &\sigma \frac{u}{1+\nu I(x_{c_B})}&R_e(x_{c_B})V_c\\
    \dot{R}_e(x) &=
    &-\sigma u&\sum\limits_{c_B}\frac{V_{c_B}}{V_D}R_e(x)\delta(x-x_{c_B})\\
    &&-\sigma\frac{u}{1+\nu I(x_{c_B})}&\sum\limits_{c_B}
    \frac{V_{c_A}}{V_D}R_e(x)\delta(x-x_{c_B}) &+& D_R\Delta R_e(x)\\
    \dot{I}(x) &= &b&\sum\limits_{c_A}\frac{V_{c_A}}{V_D}\delta(x-x_{c_A}) &+& D_I \Delta I(x).
\end{alignat}

\subsection{Distinction to Cellular Automata}

In the broader literate \acp{abm} are often treated within the same class as \acp{ca}.
However this association is superficial and one of the only shared aspects is the discrete nature of
fundamental building blocks.
This subsection explores key differences between \acp{abm} and \acp{ca}.
We illustrate these discrepancies by fully classifying elementary \acp{ca} and further study other
variations of \acp{ca}.

\begin{itemize}
    \item cellular potts model; not for cells directly but rather for sections of cells
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Elementary \aclp{ca}}

\begin{itemize}
    \item distinguish between lattice points and biological cells
    \item can be classified easily; only 256 combinations; explain this
    \item lattice points in \ac{ca} are spatial entities which are: cell occupies this space; cell
        does not occupy
    \item if we interpret lattice points as spatial distribution of cells; then we have some
        problems
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Other \aclp{ca}}

\begin{itemize}
    \item growth in \acp{ca} is always limited if density is limited
    \item conway's game of life: individual (spatial) cells are not biological cells; look at
        glider; this consists of multiple cells and only many of them together form something that
        is deemed "living"
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Conclusion}

\begin{itemize}
    \item interpreting lattice points as cells is at best a simplification
    \item does not represent real physical processes
    \item the word "cellular" in \acp{ca} refers to the spatial "cell" rather than the biological
        cell; they have not so much in common
\end{itemize}
